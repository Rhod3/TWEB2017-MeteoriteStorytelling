The goal of this website is to answer a simple question: where are we the most likely to be struck by a meteorite fall ?
We are going to try to answer this question by analysing a dataset of all known meteorite falls.

We will split our analysis in two sub-questions, accompagned by two graphs:

  • How many meteorites fall on Earth per year ?
  • Where do meteorites fall the most ?

You can either click the links above to be redirected to the appropriate section, or scroll down through the beautiful art below.

Where do meteorites fall the most ?

Now that we established that there are more and more meteorite falls as year pass, let's see where they happen

In the map below are displayed all known meteorite falls. The size of the dot is in relation with the mass of the meteorite, while the color of the dot is in a darker shade of red as the fall occured in recent years.

You can click on a country to zoom in, and hover on a dot to have some informations about the meteorite.

There are a few interesting things to observe on this map.

Firstly, it looks like a lot of the meteorite falls happenend either in East-USA, Europe, India and, proportionnaly to its size, Japan.
So, what does that mean for those countries ? Should you really avoid them if you fear an unfortunate meteorite fall ?

Well, if you only consider this dataset, yes, you should avoid them. Go live in a remote part of the world like estern China, north Canada or in the north of South America.

But let's be real and think about the nature of this dataset: it contains all the recorded meteorite falls. It means either that the fall has been observed, or that the meteorite has found afterward.
It is then totally logical that the dataset tends to have falls located in heavily populated and modern area that care about this kind of things.

So, should we really fear a meteorite fall ?

Now that we established that our dataset isn't complete enough to give a strong recommendation, let's see if it's really worth worrying about a meteorite struck.

There is only one case recorded of a human struck by a meteorite.
So, that's 1 out of 108.2 billion people that ever lived on earth.

If you compare that to the odd of winning the lottery (1 out of 139'838'160 for the Euromillions) or the odd of being struck by lightning (1 out of 300'000), a meteorite fall becomes quite unlikely.
